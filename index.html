Understanding eBooks

Understanding eBook formats

If you own a Kindle, Nook, or other e-reading device, you may never have given any thought to the format your books come in. You buy a book, it appears on your reader, and life is good. Who needs to care about file formats?

If you look a little deeper, though, you might find some reasons to care:

  • Access to more sources of books (many free!).
  • The freedom to use your books on more devices.
  • The ability to create and share your own books.
  • The freedom to lend, backup, and convert books between formats.

Proprietary eBook's

There are two big reasons that you can't read a Kindle eBook on your Nook, or a Nook book on your Kindle:

  • Technical compatability
  • Digital Right Management (or DRM)

Technical compatability simply means that they don't share much information about how their book files work. DRM is a method of scrambling (or encrypting) a file so that only the intended customer can read it, and only using hardware or software supplied by that vendor. DRM is why you can't email a Kindle book to your aunt, view a movie from the iTunes store on a Windows Phone, and even why certain DVD's only work in certain countries.

For some, DRM isn't just a practical concern, but an affront to freedom in the digital age. If you can't use a book (or song, or movie, or app) in any way you like, do you really own it?

Learn more about the fight against DRM at Defective by Design.
