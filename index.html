Understanding eBooks

Understanding eBook formats

If you own a Kindle, Nook, or other e-reading device (or purchase books from Apple's iBookstore), you may never have given any thought to the format your books come in. You buy a book, it appears on your reader, and life is good. Who needs to care about file formats?

If you look a little deeper, though, you might find some reasons to care:

  • Access to more sources of books (many free!).
  • The freedom to use your books on more devices.
  • The ability to create and share your own books.
  • The freedom to lend, backup, and convert books between formats.

Proprietary eBook's

There are two big reasons that you can't read a Kindle eBook on your Nook, or an iBook on your Kindle:

  • Technical compatability
  • Digital Right Management (or DRM)

Technical compatability simply means that they don't share much information about how their book files work. DRM is a method of scrambling (or encrypting) a file so that only the intended customer can read it, and only using hardware or software supplied by that vendor. DRM is why you can't email a Kindle book to your aunt, view a movie from the iTunes store on a Windows Phone, and even why certain DVD's only work in certain countries.

DRM also allows your access to a book to be revoked-- Kindle has done this when they lost the right to sell a book, and Nook has done this when credit cards expire.

Because of the inherent restrictions of proprietary formats and DRM, they won't be considered further in this document.

For some, DRM isn't just a practical concern, but an affront to freedom in the digital age.

Learn more about the fight against DRM at Defective by Design.

The Choices

Today, there are 3 common formats for eBooks: PDF, ePub, and Mobipocket.

PDF

Adobe's Portable Document Format. Since PDF preserves design and layout's it's great for tablets and desktop computers, and a bit awkward on phones and e-ink displays

ePub

An open standard released in 2007, and successor to Open eBook, which has been in development since 1999. ePubs can be read on most tablets, phones, computers, and eReaders, but not the Kindle.

Mobipocket

The Mobipocket company made eReader software for early handheld devices (like PDA's), and released the file format so that publishers could make use of it. Mobipocket had support for, but did not require, DRM.

Mobipocket files can be read on a Kindle, but not on many other modern devices. Mobipocket's software has been dicontinued.

Get books!

Project Gutenberg is an archive of public domain books, all available free, in many formats.

Independant publishers like OR Books and Tor Books sell books in multiple formats, and sites like Lulu.com allow any author to sell books on their marketplace.

Technology publishers like OReiily, Pragmatic, Apress, Manning, and Packt have long offered DRM-free ebooks.
